Who Wants a National Bank Charter? OCC Applications, 2014–2026

Twelve years of de novo charter and conversion applications before the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency — from the post-crisis drought, through the failed first fintech/crypto wave of 2018–2021, to the 2025–26 surge — dominated by an unprecedented wave of digital-asset and fintech filings after the OCC amended 12 CFR 5.20 (effective April 1, 2026) to clarify that national trust banks may hold assets in non-fiduciary custody. Context that makes today's queue remarkable: after the financial crisis, de novo bank formation essentially stopped — from 2010 through 2016 the industry saw only a handful of new charters nationwide, and the OCC granted almost none. Winter Park National Bank (2017) and Grasshopper (organized 2017) were the notable exceptions. The era's lasting OCC story was Comptroller Curry's December 2016 fintech-charter white paper — the intellectual ancestor of everything in the 2025–26 table above — which state regulators sued into paralysis. Twelve years later, the question "who wants a national bank charter?" has gone from almost nobody to Circle, Coinbase, Kraken, Stripe, Fidelity, Morgan Stanley-adjacent ventures, and a former Acting Comptroller on an applicant's board.

The 2018–2023 story is mostly attrition: of the crypto trust bids, only Anchorage opened (and drew a BSA consent order within 15 months); Protego's and Paxos's approvals expired unconsummated, and Figure, Monzo, Oportun and BitPay all withdrew as the OCC's posture hardened under Acting Comptroller Hsu. The exceptions proved durable: Varo (first fintech national charter, 2020), SoFi (charter via the Golden Pacific acquisition, 2022) and Agility (first MDI national charter in 15 years, 2022). Several first-wave names — Paxos, Protego, Figure-adjacent stablecoin players — are back in the 2025–26 queue.

First National Digital Currency Bank, N.A. (Circle)

De novo national trust bank · conditional approval Dec 12, 2025 · final approval July 2026 — the first digital-asset de novo trust charter to reach final approval
Newly opened: its first Call Report will be as of Sept 30, 2026 and will appear in the FFIEC CDR thereafter.
What the Call Reports tell us so far: Erebor opened with an unusually large capital cushion ($601M equity against $1.7B of assets — a 35% leverage position), is fully deposit-funded with zero loans on the books, and is parking the balance sheet in cash and securities while it builds out lending. SmartBiz shows the classic fintech-conversion pattern: small balance sheet, loan-heavy ($145M of $205M), and still working toward profitability after a –$10.1M 2025. The trust-charter applicants (Circle, Ripple, Coinbase, Paxos, BitGo, Fidelity, et al.) will never show large balance sheets — their custody business lives off-balance-sheet, which is exactly why the April 2026 amendment to 12 CFR 5.20 mattered.
